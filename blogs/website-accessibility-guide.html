Website Accessibility: Why It Matters and How to Implement It

Web accessibility (a11y) means designing and developing websites so that people with disabilities can use them. That includes visual, auditory, motor, and cognitive impairments. Beyond being a legal requirement in many countries, accessibility improves user experience for everyone and expands your audience.

Why Accessibility Matters

  • Legal compliance: The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and the European Accessibility Act mandate certain accessibility standards.
  • Market reach: Over 1 billion people worldwide live with some form of disability.
  • SEO benefits: Many accessibility practices (alt text, semantic HTML, proper heading structure) also help search engines understand your content.
  • Brand reputation: Inclusive design shows you care about all users.

WCAG 2.1 at a Glance

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are organised under four principles: Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, Robust. Key checkpoints include:

  • Perceivable: Provide text alternatives for non‑text content (alt text), captions for videos, and sufficient colour contrast.
  • Operable: Ensure all functionality is available from a keyboard, give users enough time to read content, and avoid seizure‑triggering animations.
  • Understandable: Make text readable, predict navigation, and help users avoid and correct mistakes.
  • Robust: Maximise compatibility with current and future user tools (valid HTML, ARIA).

Quick Wins to Improve Accessibility

  1. Add alt text to images. Describe the image's purpose, not just its appearance.
  2. Use semantic HTML. <button> instead of <div onclick>, <nav>, <main>, heading levels in order.
  3. Check colour contrast. Tools like WebAIM Contrast Checker help you meet WCAG AA standards.
  4. Label form inputs clearly. Use <label> elements with for attributes.
  5. Test with a keyboard. Can you navigate your site using only Tab, Shift+Tab, and Enter?

Automated Accessibility Tools

Lighthouse (built into Chrome DevTools), axe, and WAVE are excellent automated checkers. They don't catch everything, but they provide a solid starting point.
